What Made Dollar General Successful

  • Aggressive Expansion Strategy: Dollar General opened more than 700 new stores per year, demonstrating strong market penetration. This rapid growth helped them reach underserved rural and urban markets where competitors weren't present.
  • Strong Initial Margins: From 2003-2005, the company maintained a gross margin of nearly 29-30%, showing effective pricing power and cost management during their growth phase.
  • Historic Same-Store Sales Growth: Before 2006, Dollar General achieved 5-8% same-store sales growth, indicating strong customer loyalty and repeat business at existing locations.
  • Improved Inventory Efficiency: Inventory turnover improved from 3.8 to 4.7 between 2005-2007, showing better stock management and faster product movement despite operational challenges.
  • Low-Cost, High-Convenience Model: Dollar General's business model focused on small-format stores in convenient locations, serving customers who valued quick shopping trips over one-stop shopping. This differentiated them from big-box competitors like Walmart.

Challenges at Dollar General

  • Dramatic Operating Margin Collapse: Operating margin plummeted from 6.5% (2005) to 2.7% (2007)‚Äîa decline of 58%. This shows that expansion came at the cost of profitability and operational efficiency.
  • Gross Margin Compression: Gross margin fell from 29.4% to 25.8% between 2005-2007 (a 3.6 percentage point drop). This indicates rising costs, competitive pricing pressure, and a shift toward lower-margin consumable products.
  • Slowing Sales Growth: Sales growth decelerated from 12% (2005) to 6.8% (2006-2007), suggesting market saturation and diminishing returns from new store openings.
  • Weak Same-Store Sales: Same-store sales dropped from the historic 5-8% range to just 2-3%, indicating existing stores were underperforming and not generating organic growth.
  • Operational Inefficiency: The case mentions "back rooms overflowing" with inventory backlogs, showing poor space utilization and inventory management. The company grew too fast without upgrading its operational infrastructure and supply chain systems.
  • Declining Capital Efficiency: Both ROA and ROE declined during this period, meaning the company wasn't generating adequate returns on its assets and equity despite continued expansion. Growth was not translating to profitability.
